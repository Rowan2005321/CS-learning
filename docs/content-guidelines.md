# Course Content Guidelines

Open CS Atlas keeps course data small, practical, and easy to verify. A course should help learners build durable computer science knowledge, not just collect links.

## Selection Standards

- Prioritize official university open courses, public syllabi, and long-running educational projects.
- Prefer resources that are likely to remain accessible over time.
- Prefer courses with a syllabus, assignments, projects, lecture notes, labs, or problem sets.
- Mark whether the course is free or partly paid.
- Mark the primary course language.
- Mark prerequisites clearly enough for a self-learner to decide whether to start.
- Mark the last checked date for the course link and metadata.

## Strong Candidates

- University open courses from MIT, Stanford, Harvard, Carnegie Mellon, UC San Diego, Berkeley, Princeton, and similar institutions.
- Open textbooks paired with assignments or projects.
- Maintained public course websites with lecture notes and homework.
- Practice platforms only when they serve a clear roadmap role, such as interview preparation or security labs.

## Do Not Add

- Low-quality advertising pages.
- Pirated courses, reuploaded paid videos, or unofficial mirrors that violate copyright.
- Expired links or resources that require unclear sign-up flows.
- Thin listicles with no syllabus, assignments, notes, or project path.
- Courses with unverifiable provider information.

## Required Metadata

Every course entry should include:

- `id`
- `discipline`
- `level`
- `tracks`
- `provider`
- `code`
- `title.en` and `title.zh`
- `description.en` and `description.zh`
- `audience.en` and `audience.zh`
- `outcomes.en` and `outcomes.zh`
- `prerequisites.en` and `prerequisites.zh`
- `language`
- `weeks`
- `hoursPerWeek`
- `isFree`
- `officialUrl`
- `lastChecked`
- `tags`

Use `YYYY-MM-DD` for `lastChecked`.
